Research

Sustainable consumer choices & climate risk

My research agenda centers on the economics of sustainable consumer choices and climate resilience in food and agricultural systems. I focus on how households trade off multiple environmental attributes, how information about climate adaptation affects willingness to pay, and how producers and policy makers can use this information to design resilient systems.

I use a combination of discrete choice experiments, incentive compatible auctions, and spatial and bioeconomic models. Much of my work is applied to concrete cases such as tree syrups, lettuce production, drinking water contamination, and integrated pest management in field crops.

Core research areas

  • Demand for climate adapted and diversified food products
  • Tradeoffs across water, land, nitrogen, and energy footprints in food systems
  • Household willingness to pay to reduce environmental and health risks (PFAS and water quality)
  • Economic evaluation of integrated pest management strategies
  • Methodological work on non-market valuation and benefits transfer

Exploring the shelf-life of travel cost methods

Demand for climate adapted food

Becker-DeGroot-Marschak auction study on maple and birch syrups with randomized information treatments on forest diversification and climate resilience. Manuscript under review.

Environmental footprints of lettuce

Drinking water contamination and PFAS

Khan, M. J., & Halstead, J. M. Drinking water contamination in New England: Household willingness to pay to avoid PFAS exposure. Contingent valuation study of household WTP for PFAS filtration across New England states.

Integrated pest management in sweet corn

Khan, M. J., & Fan, X. A hierarchical approach to integrated pest management (IPM) in sweet corn farms. Bioeconomic modeling of pest dynamics, pesticide use, and economic thresholds.

I link theory to current policy debates and real data, often drawing directly from my research on climate adaptation, food systems, and environmental risk.
